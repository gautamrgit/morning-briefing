## 🧘 Philosophy

**On texts that survive by accident** — A fragment of Homer's Iliad turning up inside a 1,600-year-old mummy's wrappings, reused as embalming material with no apparent literary intent, is a reminder that what endures often isn't chosen for endurance. The Stoics, especially Marcus Aurelius, wrote about how little control anyone has over what of their work or effort outlasts them — legacy is rarely something engineered on purpose. For any long-range plan or strategy document, there's a useful humility in that: build for the present purpose with full seriousness, and let what proves durable prove itself, rather than trying to author your own permanence.

**On audiences that grow when outcomes stay uncertain** — Wimbledon's women's final drew nearly half again as many viewers as last year, even as the sport's biggest individual dynasties (Sinner aside) remain unsettled; the men's final, with its increasingly familiar cast, saw viewership dip. There's an old observation, going back to Aristotle's Poetics, that genuine drama depends on uncertain outcomes, not guaranteed ones — an audience's interest tracks the credible possibility that anything could happen. It's a pointed lesson for any category or brand worried that "more dominant" is always "more compelling": predictability, even when it reflects genuine excellence, can quietly cost you attention over time.
