## 🧘 Philosophy

**On recovery narratives** — Reckitt's stock story this week — cheap, buying back shares, still 29% off its high — is a case study in how markets price uncertainty differently from risk. The Stoics drew a hard line between what is "up to us" and what isn't: execution, capital discipline, and portfolio focus are within Reckitt's control; crude prices, monsoon timing and geopolitical shocks are not. Epictetus would say the July 29 results matter less as a verdict on the company than as a test of whether management is spending its energy on the right half of that ledger. For Gautam, the same discipline applies to any quarter that looks messier than the plan: separate what was foreseeable and controllable from what genuinely wasn't, and resist the urge to over-correct on the latter.

**On rivalries that don't happen** — Sinner and Zverev meet in Sunday's Wimbledon final only because Alcaraz's wrist has kept him out since April — the rivalry many expected to define this era of tennis has simply gone quiet, not resolved. There's a old idea, sharpened by Seneca, that we spend too much energy anticipating a specific future and too little building resilience for whichever future actually arrives. Sinner's excellence hasn't required Alcaraz's presence to be validated; it's been proven against whoever showed up. It's a useful corrective for competitive strategy generally — building a plan that depends on a specific rival's move (or non-move) is fragile in a way that building genuine strength is not.
