## 🧘 Philosophy

**On strategies that treat resistance as inevitable rather than avoidable** — Researchers this week described a cancer treatment approach that assumes drug resistance will eventually develop and switches therapies proactively, rather than waiting for failure to force a change. It's a strikingly practical piece of Stoic logic applied to medicine: rather than hoping a single approach holds indefinitely, plan explicitly for the point at which it won't, and build the transition in from the start. The same discipline applies to any strategy — commercial, competitive, or personal — built around a single lever; the plan that survives is usually the one that assumed its own current advantage was temporary.

**On the gains that hold up under real scrutiny** — Nestlé's 48% profit jump wasn't a low-base illusion or a one-off cost saving; management pointed to broad, specific, repeatable drivers — quick commerce, premiumisation, sharper execution — the kind of result that survives an analyst actually pulling it apart. Aristotle's distinction between apparent and genuine virtue applies just as well to a quarter's numbers: the test isn't whether a result looks good on the surface, but whether it holds together once someone asks exactly where it came from. Any results-day communication worth trusting should be built to survive that same question.
